# Why RNNs Fall Short

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) were once the default choice for handling sequence data - language, audio, time, and more. They were inspired by the idea of **'memory'** 🧠
in model; the ability to remember what came before in sequence.

But as NLP tasks become more complex and datasets grew, the limitations of RNNs became too big to ignore 😬.

<p style={{fontSize: '18px'}}> Limitation of RNNs </p>

<br/>

<OrderedList items={["Sequential Computation - No parallelism", "Vanishing and exploading gradient", "Hard to model long range dependencies"]}/>

<br/>

**Sequential Computation - No parallelism:**

RNN process input sequences one step at a time. That is, they take the first token, update the hidden state, pass that to the next token, and so on — like a chain ⛓️.


This makes parallel processing impossible during training. Even with a GPU, you can't compute step 5 without finishing step 4. As a result:

<UnorderedList items={["RNNs are slow to train, especially on long sequences. 🐌", "They don't scale well with large datasets or long inputs."]}/>

**Vanishing and Exploding Gradients:**

In deep RNNs, gradients (used for training) are multiplied through many time steps. If the values are < 1, they vanish 👻. If > 1, they explode 💥.

This lead to:

<UnorderedList items={["Difficulty in learning long-term dependenciesVanishing gradients → model 'forgets' early information in a long sequence.", "Models that can't remember what happened 10 steps ago.Exploding gradients → unstable training."]}/>

**Hard to Model Long Range Dependencies:**

Suppose a model has to answer a question like:

> "The boy who wore a blue shirt and carried a skateboard went to the park. What color was his shirt?"

The answer relies on information from earlier in the sentence.

RNNs struggle with this because their memory is compressed into a single hidden state vector, updated step-by-step. As sequences get longer, older information gets blurred or overwritten by new inputs 😵‍💫.


# Transformer Architecture - A High-Level Overview

This section introduces the Transformer's big-picture structure before diving into the individual components like attention or embeddings.

<p className='text-[18px]'>What is the Transformer</p>

The Transformer is a sequence-to-sequence model architecture introduced in the 2017 paper ***"Attention Is All You Need."*** It was designed for tasks like translation, summarization, and any problem involving a sequence of inputs and outputs.

The core idea is simple:

> Instead of processing sequences step-by-step like RNNs, Transformers look at the entire sequence at once — using attention to figure out which parts matter 🎯.

<br/>

<p className='text-[18px]'>The Two Main Parts</p>

At a high level, the Transformer is made of two main stacks:

***Decoder:***

<UnorderedList items={["Takes the encoder output and the previously generated outputs.", "Predicts the next token one by one.", "Also built from a stack of identical layers, with a few extra tweaks."]}/>

# Encoder and Decoder Blocks Components

The Transformer architecture consists of two main components: the **Encoder** and the **Decoder**. Each of these components is made up of multiple identical layers, which are stacked to form the complete architecture.

<p className="text-[18px]">Encoder Block </p>

The encoder block takes in the input sequence and creates rich representations of each word. Each encoder layer has two main parts:

<OrderedList items={["Multi-Head Self-Attention:", "Feed-Forward Network:"]} />

**Multi-Head Self-Attention:**

<UnorderedList items={["Lets the model look at all words in the sentence at the same time", "Figures out which words are important for understanding each word"]}/>

**Feed-Forward Network:**

<UnorderedList items={["Consists of two linear transformations with a ReLU activation in between", "Processes the attention results through simple calculations"]}/>

<br/>

<p className='text-[18px]'>Decoder Block</p>

The decoder block creates the output sequence, one word at a time. Each decoder layer has three main parts:
<OrderedList items={["Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention:", "Cross-Attention:", "Feed-Forward Network:"]} />

**Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention:**

<UnorderedList items={["Works like the encoder's attention, but with a twist", "It can only look at words that came before, not future words (that would be cheating! 😏)"]}/>

**Cross-Attention:**

<UnorderedList items={["This is where the magic happens - the decoder looks at what the encoder learned ✨", "It connects the input sentence with the output being generated"]}/>

**Feed-Forward Network:**

<UnorderedList items={["Same as the encoder - just processes the attention results", "Two simple layers doing some calculations"]}/>

These blocks are stacked (usually 6 or 12 layers deep), but all layers are identical in structure — just with different learned weights.


# The Attention Mechanism (Preview)

The attention block is the heart of the Transformer 💖.

At every layer, each token can attend to all other tokens — deciding how much to "focus" on each one when creating its own representation.

This attention mechanism replaces recurrence and allows the model to learn relationships regardless of distance.
